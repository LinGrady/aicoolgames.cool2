Affiliate Disclosure

Last Updated: January 15, 2026

Our Commitment to You

Hey, I'm Jake. I want to be completely transparent about how this site makes money. Building and maintaining MoneyMind takes real time and money—hosting, tools, research, and the countless hours I spend writing content that actually helps people.

Here's the honest truth: yes, this site earns money through affiliate partnerships. But I will never recommend something just because it pays a commission. My reputation and your trust are worth way more than a few bucks from a referral link.

Every product or service I mention is something I've either personally used, thoroughly researched, or would genuinely recommend to a friend. If I wouldn't use it myself, I won't recommend it to you. Period.

What Are Affiliate Links?

Affiliate links are special URLs that track when you click them and make a purchase. If you buy something through one of these links, I may earn a small commission—at no extra cost to you.

For example, if I recommend a high-yield savings account and you open an account through my link, the bank might pay me a referral fee. You get the same deal you would have gotten anyway, and I get compensated for the referral. It's a win-win.

Here's what this looks like in practice:

  • Regular link: www.bankname.com
  • Affiliate link: www.bankname.com/aff/jake123

Both take you to the same place. The affiliate link just lets the company know I sent you.

How We Make Money

MoneyMind generates revenue through several channels:

1. Affiliate Commissions

When you click certain links and make purchases or sign up for services, I may earn a commission. This includes:

  • Financial products: Credit cards, bank accounts, investment platforms
  • Software and tools: Budgeting apps, financial planning tools
  • Books and courses: Financial education resources
  • Other products: Anything else I genuinely recommend

2. Display Advertising

MoneyMind may use display advertising, including Google AdSense, after the site is approved for ads. Ads help support hosting and writing time, but they are separate from editorial decisions.

3. Sponsored Content

Occasionally, companies may pay to sponsor specific content. When this happens, you'll see a clear "Sponsored" label at the top of the article. I only accept sponsored content from companies I'd actually recommend, and the sponsorship doesn't influence my honest opinion.

Our Editorial Independence Promise

This is the most important part:

Affiliate relationships do not decide what gets published.

Here's my promise to you:

  • Honest reviews: If a product has serious drawbacks, I will say so even when it has an affiliate program.
  • Full disclosure: I'll always tell you when I have an affiliate relationship.
  • Your best interest first: My goal is to help you, not to make a quick buck.
  • No paid placements: I don't accept payment to rank products higher in my recommendations.
  • Regular reviews: I periodically re-evaluate recommendations to make sure they're still the best options.

If I recommend a product that doesn't have an affiliate program over one that does, that tells you everything about my priorities. I'd rather help you find the right solution than earn a commission on the wrong one.

How to Identify Affiliate Links

I try to make it obvious when I'm using affiliate links. Here's how you can spot them:

  • Links may have an asterisk (*) next to them
  • Product mentions might include a small disclosure like "(affiliate link)"
  • Some articles have a disclosure notice at the top
  • When in doubt, assume links could be affiliate links

But here's the thing: whether a link is affiliate or not, my recommendation is the same. The only difference is whether I get compensated for the referral.

Why Affiliate Marketing?

I could charge for access to this content. I could fill every page with annoying pop-up ads. I could sell your email address to spammers.

Instead, I choose affiliate marketing because:

  • It keeps content free: You get access to all my research and advice without paying a subscription.
  • It aligns incentives: I only make money when you find value in my recommendations enough to take action.
  • It's transparent: You know exactly how the site makes money.
  • It doesn't cost you extra: You pay the same price whether you use my link or not.

What This Means for You

Practically speaking, here's what you should know:

  1. You're never obligated to use my links. If you'd rather go directly to a company's website, that's totally fine. I'm just happy you found the information helpful.
  2. Prices are the same either way. Using my affiliate link doesn't cost you more. In some cases, I can even negotiate special deals for readers.
  3. My opinions are genuine. I've turned down lucrative affiliate partnerships because I didn't believe in the products. Your trust is worth more than any commission.
  4. I make mistakes sometimes. If I recommend something that turns out to be problematic, I'll update my content and let you know.

Questions?

If you have any questions about our affiliate relationships or how we maintain editorial integrity, I'm happy to discuss it. Transparency is important to me.

Feel free to reach out anytime. I read every email personally.

Legal Stuff

In accordance with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) guidelines, this disclosure informs you that MoneyMind may receive compensation for products or services purchased through links on this website.

The FTC requires that material connections between advertisers and endorsers be disclosed. This disclosure is made in accordance with the FTC's 16 CFR Part 255: "Guides Concerning the Use of Endorsements and Testimonials in Advertising."

For more information about the FTC's guidelines, visit FTC's Endorsement Guides.

Changes to This Disclosure

I may update this disclosure from time to time. Any changes will be posted on this page with an updated revision date. I encourage you to review this disclosure periodically to stay informed about how MoneyMind is supported.
