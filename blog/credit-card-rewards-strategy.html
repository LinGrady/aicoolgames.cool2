Important Disclaimer Before We Start

Let me be crystal clear about something: credit card rewards are NOT worth it if you carry a balance.

The average credit card interest rate is around 24%. Even the best rewards cards give you maybe 2-5% back. The math is simple: if you're paying interest, you're losing money. Period.

I only started pursuing credit card rewards AFTER I paid off my $23,000 in credit card debt and proved to myself that I could pay my balance in full every month. If you're still carrying debt, bookmark this article, pay off your cards first, then come back.

鈿狅笍 This strategy only works if:

  • You pay your balance in full every single month
  • You never spend more than you would have otherwise
  • You have a credit score above 700
  • You're organized enough to track due dates and spending

Still with me? Good. Let's talk about how I earned $3,200 in rewards last year.

My Journey: From Credit Card Disaster to Rewards Pro

In 2019, I had $23,000 in credit card debt and a credit score of 540. I was the last person who should have been thinking about rewards. My relationship with credit cards was toxic鈥擨 used them to buy things I couldn't afford, paid minimums, and watched the interest pile up.

Fast forward to 2021: I was debt-free with a credit score of 680. I'd learned to use credit cards responsibly. I paid my balance in full every month. And I started wondering: if I'm going to spend money anyway, shouldn't I get something back?

That's when I discovered the world of credit card rewards. Not the "buy everything on credit and hope for the best" approach, but a strategic, disciplined system for maximizing value without falling back into debt.

Last year (2025), I earned $3,200 in credit card rewards. Here's exactly how I did it.

Credit Card Rewards 101

Before we dive into strategy, let's cover the basics.

Types of Rewards

  • Cash Back: Simple. You get a percentage of your spending back as cash. Usually 1-5%.
  • Points: Earn points that can be redeemed for travel, gift cards, or cash. Value varies by card and redemption method.
  • Miles: Airline-specific or flexible travel rewards. Can be valuable if you travel strategically.

How Banks Make Money (And Why They Offer Rewards)

Banks aren't charities. They offer rewards because:

  1. They earn interchange fees from merchants (2-3% of every transaction)
  2. They hope you'll carry a balance and pay interest
  3. They hope you'll pay annual fees and not maximize the benefits

My goal is to take advantage of #1 while avoiding #2 and #3.

My Exact Strategy

I use a three-pronged approach:

1. Sign-Up Bonuses (The Big Wins)

Most of my rewards came from sign-up bonuses. Many cards offer $200-$1,000+ worth of points or cash back when you spend a certain amount in the first 3 months.

Last year, I opened 4 cards with sign-up bonuses worth a combined $2,100. But here's the key: I only opened cards when I had natural, planned spending that would meet the minimum spend requirement.

2. Category Spending (The Steady Earn)

I use different cards for different categories to maximize cash back:

  • Groceries: 6% back with my American Express Blue Cash Preferred
  • Dining: 3% back with my Chase Freedom Flex
  • Travel: 3% back with my Capital One Venture
  • Everything else: 2% back with my Citi Double Cash

This earned me about $800 in ongoing rewards last year.

3. Strategic Redemptions

For points cards, I maximize value by:

  • Transferring points to airline partners for international flights (often 2+ cents per point)
  • Using points for flights, not merchandise or gift cards
  • Waiting for transfer bonuses (sometimes 30-50% extra)

Sign-Up Bonuses: The Real Money Maker

Here's where I earned the bulk of my $3,200. Sign-up bonuses are the fastest way to earn rewards, but they require discipline.

My Sign-Up Bonus Strategy

  1. Only open cards when I have planned spending: I don't manufacture spending. I wait until I have natural expenses (car insurance, travel, holiday shopping) that will meet the minimum.
  2. Never more than 2 new cards every 3 months: This keeps my credit score stable and ensures I can manage the spending requirements.
  3. Track everything in a spreadsheet: Card name, open date, minimum spend, deadline, bonus earned, annual fee.
  4. Set calendar reminders: For minimum spend deadlines and annual fee renewal dates.
  5. Keep cards open for at least a year: Closing cards too quickly can hurt your credit score.

Mistakes I Made (So You Don't Have To)

Mistake #1: Buying Things Just to Hit Minimum Spend

Early on, I bought a $300 gadget I didn't need to hit a minimum spend. The bonus was $200. I lost $100. Lesson: Only spend what you would have spent anyway.

Mistake #2: Missing a Payment

I forgot about a card I rarely used and missed the payment. Late fee: $30. Interest: $12. Credit score hit: 15 points. Lesson: Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every card.

Mistake #3: Paying Annual Fees for Cards I Didn't Use

I kept a card with a $95 annual fee for two years without using it once. That's $190 wasted. Lesson: Review cards annually. If you're not getting more value than the fee, downgrade or cancel.

Mistake #4: Redeeming Points for Gift Cards

I once redeemed 20,000 points for a $200 gift card. Later I learned those same points could have been a $400 flight. Lesson: Learn the best redemption options before cashing out.

Mistake #5: Applying for Too Many Cards Too Fast

I applied for 5 cards in 2 months. My credit score dropped 40 points. Lesson: Space out applications. I now do max 2 per quarter.

My 5 Non-Negotiable Rules

These rules have kept me from falling back into debt while earning rewards:

  1. Never carry a balance. If I can't pay for it in cash today, I can't put it on a credit card. No exceptions.
  2. Never spend more to earn rewards. A 5% bonus on $100 of unnecessary spending is still losing $95.
  3. Track every card in a spreadsheet. Due dates, annual fees, bonus deadlines, current balance.
  4. Autopay everything. At least the minimum, ideally the full balance. Never trust my memory.
  5. Review cards annually. Keep only what provides more value than the annual fee.

Should You Try This?

Credit card rewards aren't for everyone. Here's how to know if you're ready:

You're Ready If:

  • 鉁?You pay your credit cards in full every month
  • 鉁?You have a credit score above 700
  • 鉁?You're organized and can track multiple cards
  • 鉁?You have stable income and spending
  • 鉁?You won't be tempted to overspend

You're NOT Ready If:

  • 鉂?You carry a balance on any credit card
  • 鉂?You've missed payments in the last year
  • 鉂?Your credit score is below 680
  • 鉂?You're impulsive with spending
  • 鉂?You're planning to apply for a mortgage soon

Where to Start

If you're new to rewards, start simple:

  1. Get one good 2% cash back card (Citi Double Cash, Wells Fargo Active Cash, or PayPal Mastercard)
  2. Use it for everything for 6 months
  3. Prove to yourself you can pay in full every month
  4. Then consider adding category cards or pursuing sign-up bonuses

The Bottom Line

Credit card rewards can be a great way to earn extra money on spending you'd do anyway. Last year, I earned $3,200鈥攅ssentially free money for being strategic about which cards I use.

But here's the truth: if you're paying interest, rewards don't matter. The 24% interest rate destroys any benefit from 2-5% rewards. Pay off your debt first. Build good habits. Then, and only then, consider optimizing for rewards.

The best rewards card is the one you pay in full every month. Everything else is secondary.
