College Savings 101: 529 Plans and Beyond

✍️ By Sarah Johnson, CFP 📅 December 10, 2023 ⏱️ 12 min read

The cost of college has skyrocketed over the past few decades. According to the College Board, the average cost of tuition and fees for the 2023-2024 school year was $11,260 for public in-state colleges and $41,540 for private colleges—and that's before room, board, books, and other expenses.

For a child born today, four years at a public university could cost over $200,000. Private college could exceed $400,000. The good news? With proper planning and the right savings vehicles, you can significantly reduce the burden of college costs.

How Much Do You Need to Save?

Before choosing a savings strategy, estimate your target amount:

Projected College Costs (4 Years):

College Type Current Cost Projected 2035
Public In-State $100,000 $180,000
Public Out-of-State $160,000 $288,000
Private College $240,000 $432,000

Don't panic—you don't need to save 100% of these amounts. Most families use a combination of savings, current income, scholarships, grants, and student loans.

The One-Third Rule:

A common guideline suggests covering college costs through three sources:

  • One-third from savings and investments
  • One-third from current income while child is in college
  • One-third from financial aid, scholarships, and loans

College Savings Options Compared

1. 529 Plans (The Gold Standard)

529 plans are the most popular college savings vehicle, and for good reason. They offer significant tax advantages and flexibility.

How 529 Plans Work:

  • Contributions grow tax-free
  • Withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses
  • Some states offer tax deductions for contributions
  • High contribution limits (often $300,000+ per beneficiary)
  • No income limits

Qualified Expenses Include:

  • Tuition and fees
  • Room and board
  • Books and supplies
  • Computers and internet
  • K-12 tuition (up to $10,000/year)
  • Student loan repayment (up to $10,000 lifetime)
  • Apprenticeship programs

Types of 529 Plans:

Type How It Works Best For
Prepaid Tuition Lock in today's rates for future tuition Certain about in-state public college
Education Savings Invest in mutual funds, growth potential Most families

Choosing a 529 Plan:

You can invest in any state's 529 plan, not just your own. Consider:

  • Your state's tax benefits: Some states offer deductions only for in-state plans
  • Investment options: Look for low-cost index funds
  • Fees: Lower is better; aim for under 0.5%
  • Performance: Compare historical returns

Top-Rated 529 Plans:

  • Utah (my529) - Low fees, excellent investment options
  • Virginia (Invest529) - Low costs, age-based options
  • California (ScholarShare) - Diverse investment choices
  • New York (Direct Plan) - Low fees, Vanguard funds
  • Michigan (Education Savings Program) - Low costs, good performance

529 Plan Contribution Limits:

  • No annual federal contribution limit
  • Annual gift tax exclusion: $18,000 per person (2024)
  • 5-year gift averaging: Contribute up to $90,000 ($180,000 for couples) in one year without gift tax consequences
  • Aggregate limits vary by state: $235,000 to $550,000

What If Your Child Doesn't Go to College?

You have options:

  • Change beneficiary: Transfer to another family member
  • Use for other education: Trade school, community college, graduate school
  • Save for grandchildren: Keep the account for future generations
  • Withdraw with penalty: Pay taxes plus 10% penalty on earnings (contributions are never taxed)

2. Coverdell Education Savings Accounts (ESAs)

Coverdell ESAs offer similar tax benefits to 529 plans but with more restrictions.

Features:

  • Tax-free growth and withdrawals for education
  • Can be used for K-12 expenses (not just college)
  • More investment flexibility than most 529 plans
  • Account must be used by age 30 or transferred

Limitations:

  • Contribution limit: $2,000 per year per beneficiary
  • Income limits: Phase out at $95,000-$110,000 single, $190,000-$220,000 married
  • No state tax deductions

Verdict: Consider a Coverdell only if you've maxed out 529 contributions and want additional K-12 flexibility.

3. Custodial Accounts (UGMA/UTMA)

Uniform Gifts to Minors Act (UGMA) and Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (UTMA) accounts are taxable investment accounts held in a child's name.

Pros:

  • No restrictions on how money is used
  • Some tax advantages (first $1,250 of unearned income is tax-free, next $1,250 taxed at child's rate)
  • Simple to set up

Cons:

  • Assets legally belong to the child at age of majority (18-21)
  • Child can use money for anything—not just education
  • Counts heavily against financial aid (20% vs. 5.64% for parent assets)
  • No tax-free growth like 529 plans

Verdict: Generally not recommended for college savings due to financial aid impact and lack of restrictions.

4. Roth IRA

While primarily a retirement account, Roth IRAs can double as a college savings vehicle.

Pros:

  • Contributions can be withdrawn anytime, tax and penalty-free
  • Earnings can be withdrawn penalty-free for education (but taxed)
  • If not needed for college, keeps growing for retirement
  • Doesn't count as asset for financial aid purposes

Cons:

  • Low contribution limit: $7,000/year ($8,000 if 50+)
  • Income limits for contributions
  • Using for college reduces retirement savings

Verdict: Good backup option or supplement to 529 plans, especially if retirement savings are on track.

5. Taxable Brokerage Account

A regular investment account offers maximum flexibility.

Pros:

  • No contribution limits
  • No restrictions on use
  • Tax-loss harvesting opportunities
  • Qualified dividends and long-term capital gains at favorable rates

Cons:

  • No tax-free growth
  • Capital gains taxes on sales
  • Must manage tax implications

Verdict: Consider if you've maxed out tax-advantaged options or want maximum flexibility.

Investment Strategy for College Savings

Age-Based Portfolios:

Most 529 plans offer age-based portfolios that automatically adjust as your child gets older:

  • Ages 0-6: Aggressive (80-100% stocks)
  • Ages 7-12: Moderate (60-80% stocks)
  • Ages 13-17: Conservative (20-40% stocks)
  • Age 18+: Very conservative (0-20% stocks)

Static Portfolios:

If you prefer more control, choose a static allocation and manually adjust over time. Common options include:

  • Aggressive Growth (100% stocks)
  • Growth (80% stocks, 20% bonds)
  • Moderate (60% stocks, 40% bonds)
  • Conservative (20% stocks, 80% bonds)
  • Money Market/Stable Value (100% cash/bonds)

Maximizing Financial Aid

Even with savings, most families qualify for some financial aid. Strategies to maximize aid:

1. Understand the FAFSA

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid determines eligibility for:

  • Federal grants (free money)
  • Work-study programs
  • Federal student loans

2. Know What Counts Against You

FAFSA formula:

  • Parent assets: Assessed at up to 5.64%
  • Student assets: Assessed at 20%
  • Parent income: Assessed at up to 47%
  • Student income: Assessed at 50% over protection allowance

3. Strategic Asset Placement

  • Keep assets in parents' names, not child's
  • Pay off consumer debt before filing FAFSA
  • Maximize retirement contributions (retirement assets don't count)
  • Spend down student assets before filing

4. Time Income Strategically

Base year for FAFSA is the year before college starts. Avoid:

  • Capital gains
  • Roth conversions
  • Bonuses or extra income

How Much Should You Save Monthly?

Monthly contributions needed to reach $100,000 by age 18:

Starting Age Monthly at 6% Return Monthly at 8% Return
Newborn $256 $208
Age 5 $406 $343
Age 10 $780 $700
Age 15 $2,400 $2,300

The lesson: Start early! Even small contributions early on can grow significantly thanks to compound interest.

Getting Started: Action Steps

  1. Estimate future costs based on your child's age and desired college type
  2. Set a savings goal (aim for one-third of projected costs)
  3. Choose a 529 plan (start with your state's plan if it offers tax benefits)
  4. Set up automatic contributions (even $100/month helps)
  5. Ask family to contribute (birthdays, holidays, special occasions)
  6. Increase contributions when you get raises or bonuses
  7. Review and adjust annually

Key Takeaways

  • 529 plans are the best option for most families due to tax benefits and flexibility
  • Start as early as possible—time is your greatest ally
  • You don't need to save 100%—aim for one-third and supplement with other sources
  • Use age-based portfolios to automatically adjust risk over time
  • Maximize financial aid by understanding the FAFSA formula
  • Ask for contributions from family instead of toys and clothes
  • Automate your savings to stay consistent

College savings can feel overwhelming, but every dollar you save is a dollar your child won't have to borrow. Start with what you can afford, even if it's just $50 or $100 per month. The most important step is simply beginning. Your future college student will thank you.
