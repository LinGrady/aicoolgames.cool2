How I Think About a Checking-Account Buffer

By Jake Miller | Updated June 22, 2026 | 6 min read

A checking-account buffer is not an emergency fund. It is the little cushion that keeps normal timing problems from becoming emergencies: a bill posts a day early, payroll lands after lunch instead of midnight, or groceries cost more because two household staples ran out together.

My rule: boring money should stay boring

I like a buffer because it reduces the number of times I have to make a dramatic decision. Without one, I used to move money from savings for normal bills, then promise to put it back later. That blurred the line between planned spending and actual emergencies.

How much is enough?

For many people, a starter buffer of $100 to $300 changes the week. A larger buffer might be one week of basic expenses. I would not build a huge checking buffer before starting an emergency fund, because checking accounts usually tempt spending more than savings accounts do.

How to build it without feeling broke

I built mine with leftover category money, small refunds, and the occasional skipped purchase. If I had $18 left in dining out, I moved $10 to the buffer and left $8 alone. Small moves worked better than waiting for a perfect month.

Protect the buffer with a name

Calling it "extra money" made it disappear. Calling it "do not touch below $250" made it a rule. Some people keep the buffer as an invisible floor in checking; others move it to a separate savings pocket. Either can work if the purpose is clear.
