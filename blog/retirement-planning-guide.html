My Story: Starting Late

I didn't start saving for retirement until I was 29. Before that, I was too busy drowning in debt and living paycheck to paycheck. Retirement felt like a problem for "future Jake" to worry about.

Big mistake. Let me show you the math.

If I had started saving $500/month at age 25 instead of 29, with an average 7% return, I'd have an extra $180,000 by age 65. That four-year delay cost me nearly two hundred grand. And that's just the delay鈥擨 also missed out on years of employer matching and compound growth.

But here's the good news: it's never too late to start. I'm 35 now, and I've built a solid retirement plan. I won't retire at 50 like some FIRE bloggers, but I'm on track for a comfortable retirement at 65. This article is everything I wish I'd known at 25.

Retirement Account Types Explained

Let's break down the main retirement account types in the US:

401(k) / 403(b) (Employer-Sponsored)

  • 2026 Contribution Limit: $23,000 (plus $7,500 catch-up if 50+)
  • Tax Treatment: Traditional (pre-tax) or Roth (after-tax)
  • Employer Match: Often 3-6% of salary鈥攖his is FREE MONEY
  • Investment Options: Limited to what your plan offers
  • Best For: Getting that employer match, high earners

Traditional IRA

  • 2026 Contribution Limit: $7,000 (plus $1,000 catch-up if 50+)
  • Tax Treatment: Contributions may be tax-deductible; withdrawals taxed as income
  • Income Limits: Deduction limited if you have a workplace plan
  • Investment Options: Almost anything (stocks, bonds, ETFs, etc.)
  • Best For: Lower income years, tax deduction now

Roth IRA

  • 2026 Contribution Limit: $7,000 (plus $1,000 catch-up if 50+)
  • Tax Treatment: Contributions after-tax; withdrawals TAX-FREE in retirement
  • Income Limits: Phase-out starts at $146,000 (single) or $230,000 (married)
  • Investment Options: Almost anything
  • Best For: Young people, expect higher taxes in retirement, tax diversification

HSA (Health Savings Account)

Surprise! This is actually the best retirement account you've never heard of.

  • Triple Tax Advantage: Tax-deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses
  • After 65: Can withdraw for any reason (just pay income tax, like a 401k)
  • 2026 Limit: $4,150 (single) or $8,300 (family)
  • Requirement: Must have a high-deductible health plan
  • Best For: Anyone with an HDHP鈥攎ax this out before taxable investing

My Current Retirement Strategy

Here's exactly what I do, in order of priority:

Step 1: Get the Full Employer Match (Free Money)

My employer matches 100% of the first 3% I contribute, plus 50% of the next 2%. That's essentially a 4% raise. I contribute 5% to get the full match.

Never leave match money on the table. This is a 50-100% return on your contribution.

Step 2: Max Out HSA

I have a high-deductible health plan, so I contribute the family max ($8,300) to my HSA. I don't use it for current medical expenses鈥擨 pay those out of pocket and let the HSA grow tax-free for retirement.

Step 3: Max Out Roth IRA

$7,000/year to my Roth IRA. I like the tax-free growth and flexibility (can withdraw contributions penalty-free if needed).

Step 4: Increase 401(k) Contributions

After maxing Roth IRA, I increase 401(k) contributions. I'm not at the $23,000 max yet鈥擨'm working up to it. Currently at $15,000/year.

Step 5: Taxable Brokerage

Any extra savings goes into a regular taxable brokerage account. No tax advantages, but no contribution limits or withdrawal restrictions.

How Much Should You Save?

The standard advice is 10-15% of your income. But let's be more specific:

If You Start at 25:

  • 10% of income = comfortable retirement
  • 15% of income = early retirement possible
  • 20%+ of income = FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early)

If You Start at 35 (Like Me):

  • 15% of income = comfortable retirement
  • 20% of income = on track for solid retirement
  • 25%+ of income = catching up and accelerating

If You Start at 45:

  • 20% of income = playing catch-up
  • 25-30% of income = getting on track
  • Max out all accounts + taxable = best shot at comfortable retirement

馃挕 Quick Rule of Thumb: Multiply your age by your annual income, then divide by 10. That's a rough target for where your retirement savings should be. At 35 with a $72,000 income, my target is (35 脳 72,000) / 10 = $252,000. I'm at $143,000, so I'm behind鈥攂ut catching up.

What to Invest In

Asset allocation matters more than picking individual stocks. Here's my simple approach:

My Portfolio (Age 35)

  • 80% Stocks / 20% Bonds (will shift to 70/30 at 40, 60/40 at 50)
  • Stocks: 70% US Total Market, 30% International
  • Bonds: Total Bond Market

Specific Funds I Use

  • US Stocks: VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF) or FZROX (Fidelity ZERO Total Market)
  • International: VTIAX (Vanguard Total International) or FTIHX (Fidelity Total International)
  • Bonds: BND (Vanguard Total Bond Market) or FXNAX (Fidelity US Bond Index)

Why Not Individual Stocks?

I used to pick individual stocks. I thought I was smart. I wasn't. I underperformed the market consistently. Now I stick to index funds. Boring, but effective.

Warren Buffett's advice for most investors: "Consistently buy a low-cost S&P 500 index fund." That's what I do.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Not Getting the Full Employer Match

This is leaving free money on the table. Always contribute at least enough to get the full match.

Mistake #2: Cashing Out When Changing Jobs

When you leave a job, you can cash out your 401(k). DON'T. You'll pay taxes plus a 10% penalty. Roll it over to your new 401(k) or an IRA.

Mistake #3: Being Too Conservative

If you're 30 with all your retirement money in bonds or a money market fund, you're losing to inflation. Take appropriate risk for your age.

Mistake #4: Being Too Aggressive

Conversely, if you're 55 with 100% in crypto or tech stocks, one bad year could devastate your retirement. Diversify.

Mistake #5: Ignoring Fees

A 1% fee difference might not sound like much, but over 30 years, it can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars. Look for expense ratios under 0.2%.

Mistake #6: Trying to Time the Market

I tried this. I lost money. Now I invest the same amount every month regardless of what the market is doing. Dollar-cost averaging works.

Mistake #7: Not Increasing Contributions with Raises

Every time you get a raise, increase your retirement contribution by at least 1%. You won't miss money you never had.

Catching Up: What to Do If You're Behind

If you're reading this thinking "I'm 40 and have nothing saved," don't panic. Here's your catch-up plan:

Step 1: Start Today

Open a Roth IRA and set up automatic contributions. Even $100/month is better than zero.

Step 2: Take Advantage of Catch-Up Contributions

At 50+, you can contribute extra to 401(k)s and IRAs. Use this.

Step 3: Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts

Before investing in taxable accounts, max out all tax-advantaged options: 401(k), IRA, HSA.

Step 4: Consider Working Longer

Working just 2-3 extra years can significantly improve your retirement outlook. More savings, more Social Security, fewer years to fund.

Step 5: Delay Social Security

Waiting until 70 to claim Social Security increases your benefit by 8% per year after full retirement age. That's a guaranteed 8% return.

Step 6: Reduce Retirement Expenses

Downsizing, relocating to a lower cost-of-living area, or paying off your mortgage can dramatically reduce how much you need in retirement.

Your Action Plan

Here's exactly what to do this week:

Day 1: Check Your 401(k) Match

Log into your 401(k) account. Find out what your employer match is. Make sure you're contributing enough to get the full match.

Day 2: Open a Roth IRA

If you don't have one, open a Roth IRA at Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab. All three are excellent with low fees.

Day 3: Set Up Automatic Contributions

Automate everything. Set up automatic contributions to your 401(k) and IRA. You shouldn't have to think about this every month.

Day 4: Choose Your Investments

For most people, a simple three-fund portfolio works: US Total Market, International, Bonds. Pick low-cost index funds.

Day 5: Check Your Fees

Look at the expense ratios of your funds. If they're over 0.5%, consider switching to lower-cost options.

Day 6: Calculate Your Number

Use a retirement calculator (I like the one at Personal Capital or Fidelity) to see if you're on track. If not, increase contributions.

Day 7: Set a Reminder

Put a recurring event on your calendar to review your retirement accounts annually. I do mine every January.

The Bottom Line

Retirement planning isn't complicated, but it requires consistency. Start now, even if it's small. Increase contributions over time. Keep costs low. Stay the course.

I started late, and I'll probably work until 65. But I'm on track for a comfortable retirement. If you're just starting, you can be too.

The best time to start was 20 years ago. The second best time is today.
