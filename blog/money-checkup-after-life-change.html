The 45-Minute Money Checkup I Use After a Life Change

Why I Do a Money Checkup

A budget can look great in January and be useless by June. That is not because you failed. It is usually because life moved and the spreadsheet did not. A new job changes commuting costs. A move changes utilities. A baby changes everything. Even a small raise can quietly disappear if it lands at the same time as higher rent, a new insurance premium, or a few subscriptions you forgot about.

I learned this the annoying way. After one job change, I kept using the old budget for almost two months. My paycheck was slightly bigger, so I assumed I had breathing room. What I had actually done was add parking, lunches near the office, a higher phone plan, and a gym membership near work. The raise was real, but the extra room was mostly imaginary.

Now I do a short money checkup whenever something meaningful changes. I do not try to rebuild my whole financial life in one sitting. I just want to know three things: what changed, what the new normal costs, and what decision deserves attention first.

Step 1: Write Down What Changed

Start with a plain list. Do not open ten tabs or make this a research project. Write down the life change and the money areas it touches.

Examples:

  • New job: income, taxes, commuting, clothes, lunch, benefits, retirement match.
  • Moving: rent, utilities, deposits, internet, insurance, transportation, furniture.
  • Getting married: shared bills, account setup, debt payments, insurance, goals.
  • New baby: medical bills, childcare, diapers, formula, leave, emergency fund.
  • Debt paid off: freed-up payment, savings target, retirement contribution, lifestyle creep.

The point is to make the invisible visible. A life change usually comes with a few obvious expenses and several quiet ones. The quiet ones are what sneak into the budget and make you wonder why nothing feels easier.

Step 2: Rebuild the Monthly Baseline

Next, make a rough version of your new monthly baseline. This is not the dream budget. It is the honest version of what your life costs right now.

I use five buckets:

  • Income: take-home pay, side work, predictable support, and any recurring money coming in.
  • Must-pay bills: housing, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments, phone, internet, transportation.
  • Variable basics: groceries, gas, household supplies, medicine, school costs, pet costs.
  • Irregular costs: car registration, annual subscriptions, gifts, travel, repairs, medical deductibles.
  • Goals: emergency fund, debt payoff, investing, sinking funds, planned purchases.

If you only have 45 minutes, use the last full month of bank and credit card activity. It will not be perfect, but it is better than guessing. I usually round numbers up a little for categories that swing around, because a budget that only works in a perfect month is not really a budget.

One useful question: "If next month looked exactly like last month, would I be okay?" If the answer is no, the checkup has already done its job. You found the pressure before it turned into a late fee or a credit card balance.

Step 3: Check the Leaks

After a life change, I look for leaks before I look for big dramatic cuts. Leaks are small expenses that made sense for a minute but stopped earning their place.

Look for:

  • Duplicate subscriptions after a move or marriage.
  • Delivery fees that grew during a busy season and never went back down.
  • Insurance, phone, or internet plans that no longer match usage.
  • Automatic savings that are too high for the new cash flow, or too low after debt is paid off.
  • Old memberships tied to a previous neighborhood, job, or routine.

I do not cancel everything. That usually creates a budget that feels punished and breaks in two weeks. I pick two or three changes that create real breathing room. Canceling a forgotten subscription is nice. Changing an expensive habit you repeat five times a week is better.

Step 4: Reset the Next 90 Days

A life change does not require a five-year financial plan by Friday. Most of the time, the next 90 days matter more. Choose one main job for your money during this transition.

Good 90-day goals sound like this:

  • "Rebuild the emergency fund back to $2,000 after moving costs."
  • "Keep dining out under $300 while we adjust to the new commute."
  • "Put the old car payment toward credit card debt every payday."
  • "Save the first three months of the raise instead of upgrading fixed expenses."
  • "Price childcare, insurance, and medical costs before changing retirement contributions."

The goal should be boring enough that you can actually do it. Big dramatic plans are exciting for one evening. Small clear plans are what protect your bank account on a random Thursday.

A Simple Script for the Review

If you want a quick version, open a note and answer these six questions:

  1. What changed in my life or income?
  2. Which monthly bills are different now?
  3. Which costs are temporary, and which are the new normal?
  4. What expense surprised me when I reviewed last month?
  5. What one category needs a firmer limit for the next 90 days?
  6. What automatic transfer or payment should I adjust today?

That is enough. You can always make the budget prettier later. The first job is to stop living with outdated numbers.

Final Thought

A money checkup is not about blaming yourself for spending. It is about noticing when your financial map no longer matches the road. Life changes are already stressful. A short review gives you a cleaner picture, a few decisions you can make now, and less chance of being surprised by your own checking account.
