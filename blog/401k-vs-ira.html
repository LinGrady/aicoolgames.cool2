401(k) vs. IRA: Where Should You Save for Retirement?

✍️ By Jake Miller 📅 December 22, 2023 ⏱️ 12 min read

Planning for retirement is one of the most important financial decisions you'll make. But with so many retirement account options—401(k)s, traditional IRAs, Roth IRAs—it can be confusing to know where to put your money. Should you max out your 401(k)? Open an IRA? Do both?

This practical guide will break down the differences between these accounts, explain their unique benefits, and help you create a retirement savings strategy that maximizes your wealth.

Understanding 401(k) Plans

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement plan. If your employer offers one, you can contribute directly from your paycheck, often with tax benefits.

Key Features of 401(k)s:

  • Employer-sponsored: Only available through your job
  • High contribution limits: $23,000 per year in 2026 ($30,500 if age 50+)
  • Employer matching: Many employers match your contributions (free money!)
  • Tax-deferred growth: Traditional 401(k)s reduce taxable income now; Roth 401(k)s provide tax-free withdrawals later
  • Limited investment options: You must choose from your employer's selected funds
  • Loan options: Some plans allow you to borrow from your balance

Understanding IRAs

An IRA (Individual Retirement Account) is opened by you, not your employer. You have more control and investment options, but lower contribution limits.

Types of IRAs:

Traditional IRA

  • Contributions may be tax-deductible
  • Grows tax-deferred
  • Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income
  • Required minimum distributions (RMDs) start at age 73

Roth IRA

  • Contributions are made with after-tax dollars (no immediate tax break)
  • Grows tax-free
  • Qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free
  • No required minimum distributions
  • Can withdraw contributions (not earnings) anytime without penalty

Traditional vs. Roth: Which Is Better?

The fundamental question is: Do you want the tax break now (Traditional) or in retirement (Roth)?

Choose Traditional If:

  • You're in a high tax bracket now and expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement
  • You need the tax deduction to reduce your current taxable income
  • You want to lower your adjusted gross income (AGI) for other tax benefits
  • You expect your income to decrease significantly in retirement

Choose Roth If:

  • You're in a lower tax bracket now and expect to be in a higher bracket in retirement
  • You want tax-free income in retirement
  • You want flexibility (can withdraw contributions anytime)
  • You want to avoid required minimum distributions
  • You want to leave tax-free money to heirs

The Case for Both

Many financial experts recommend having both Traditional and Roth accounts. This gives you "tax diversification"—the flexibility to choose which account to withdraw from based on your tax situation in retirement.

Where Should You Save First? The Priority Order

Step 1: Contribute Enough to Get Full 401(k) Match

If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute at least enough to get the full match. This is free money—an immediate 50-100% return on your investment.

Example: If your employer matches 50% up to 6% of your salary, and you earn $60,000:

  • Contribute 6% ($3,600) to get the full match ($1,800)
  • Total contribution: $5,400
  • Your cost: $3,600
  • Immediate return: 50%

Step 2: Max Out Your HSA (If Eligible)

If you have a high-deductible health plan, max out your Health Savings Account (HSA) before other retirement accounts. HSAs offer triple tax advantages:

  • Tax-deductible contributions
  • Tax-free growth
  • Tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses (at any age)

After age 65, you can withdraw for any purpose (taxed like a traditional 401(k)).

Step 3: Max Out Your IRA

Next, contribute up to the IRA limit ($7,000 in 2026). Choose Roth or Traditional based on your tax situation.

IRAs typically offer:

  • Lower fees than many 401(k)s
  • More investment options
  • Greater flexibility and control

Step 4: Max Out Your 401(k)

If you still have money to save, go back to your 401(k) and increase contributions until you hit the annual limit ($23,000 in 2026).

Step 5: Taxable Brokerage Account

If you've maxed out all tax-advantaged accounts and still want to save more, open a taxable brokerage account. While you don't get tax breaks, you have complete flexibility to withdraw anytime.

Common Retirement Account Mistakes to Avoid

1. Not Getting the Full Employer Match

This is leaving free money on the table. Always contribute at least enough to get the full match before contributing elsewhere.

2. Cashing Out When Changing Jobs

When you leave a job, don't cash out your 401(k). You'll pay income tax plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty. Instead, roll it over to your new 401(k) or an IRA.

3. Ignoring Fees

High fees can erode your returns significantly over time. Compare expense ratios and choose low-cost index funds when available.

4. Being Too Conservative (or Too Aggressive)

Your investment mix should match your time horizon. Young investors can afford more risk; those near retirement should be more conservative.

5. Not Increasing Contributions

Increase your contribution rate with every raise. If you get a 3% raise, increase your contribution by 1-2%. You won't feel the difference, but your future self will thank you.

Special Considerations

Self-Employed? Consider a Solo 401(k) or SEP IRA

If you're self-employed, you have even more options:

  • Solo 401(k): Contribute up to $69,000 in 2026 ($76,500 if 50+)
  • SEP IRA: Contribute up to 25% of compensation, max $69,000
  • SIMPLE IRA: For small businesses with employees

The Backdoor Roth IRA

If you earn too much to contribute directly to a Roth IRA, you can use the "backdoor" method:

  1. Contribute to a Traditional IRA (non-deductible)
  2. Immediately convert to a Roth IRA
  3. Pay taxes only on any growth (minimal if done quickly)

This allows high earners to access Roth benefits regardless of income limits.

Key Takeaways

  • Always get your full employer 401(k) match—it's free money
  • Choose Roth if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement
  • Choose Traditional if you need the tax deduction now
  • Max out IRAs for lower fees and more investment options
  • Aim to save 15-20% of your income for retirement
  • Start as early as possible—the power of compound growth is incredible

The best retirement account is the one you'll actually contribute to consistently. Start with your employer match, then build from there. Your future retired self will thank you for every dollar you save today.
