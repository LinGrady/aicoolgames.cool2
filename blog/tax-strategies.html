Tax Planning

Tax Strategies to Keep More of Your Money

✍️ By Jake Miller 📅 December 12, 2023 ⏱️ 11 min read

Taxes are likely your largest expense. For many households, federal, state, and local taxes consume 20-40% of income. But here's the good news: the tax code is filled with legal opportunities to reduce what you owe.

This guide covers proven tax strategies that can save you hundreds or even thousands of dollars each year. These aren't loopholes or shady schemes—these are legitimate strategies built into the tax code to encourage behaviors like saving for retirement, homeownership, and education.

Understanding Your Tax Bracket

Before diving into strategies, you need to understand how tax brackets work. The U.S. has a progressive tax system—you pay higher rates only on income above each threshold.

2024 Federal Tax Brackets (Single Filers):

Tax Rate Income Range
10% $0 - $11,600
12% $11,601 - $47,150
22% $47,151 - $100,525
24% $100,526 - $191,950
32% $191,951 - $243,725
35% $243,726 - $609,350
37% Over $609,350

Key Point: If you're in the 22% bracket, you don't pay 22% on all your income—you pay 10% on the first $11,600, 12% on the next $35,550, and 22% only on income above $47,150.

Strategy 1: Maximize Tax-Advantaged Accounts

The most powerful tax strategy is using accounts that offer tax benefits. Here's the priority order:

1. 401(k) or 403(b)

  • Contribute up to $23,000 in 2024 ($30,500 if age 50+)
  • Traditional contributions reduce taxable income now
  • Roth contributions provide tax-free withdrawals later
  • Many employers offer matching contributions (free money!)

2. Traditional or Roth IRA

  • Contribute up to $7,000 in 2024 ($8,000 if age 50+)
  • Traditional: Tax deduction now, taxed on withdrawal
  • Roth: No deduction now, tax-free growth and withdrawals

3. Health Savings Account (HSA)

If you have a high-deductible health plan, the HSA offers triple tax advantages:

  • Tax-deductible contributions
  • Tax-free growth
  • Tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses

2024 limits: $4,150 individual, $8,300 family ($1,000 catch-up if 55+)

4. 529 College Savings Plans

  • Tax-free growth for education expenses
  • Some states offer tax deductions for contributions
  • Can now use for K-12 tuition (up to $10,000/year)

5. Flexible Spending Accounts (FSA)

  • Healthcare FSA: Up to $3,200 in 2024 (use it or lose it)
  • Dependent Care FSA: Up to $5,000 for childcare
  • Reduces taxable income

Strategy 2: Choose the Right Deductions

Each year, you can choose between the standard deduction or itemizing deductions. Choose whichever is larger.

2024 Standard Deduction:

  • Single: $14,600
  • Married Filing Jointly: $29,200
  • Head of Household: $21,900

Common Itemized Deductions:

  • Mortgage interest: On loans up to $750,000
  • State and local taxes (SALT): Up to $10,000
  • Charitable contributions: Cash and property donations
  • Medical expenses: Amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI

Bunching Strategy:

If your itemized deductions are close to the standard deduction, consider "bunching"—concentrating deductions in alternate years:

  • Year 1: Make two years of charitable donations, pay property taxes early
  • Year 2: Take standard deduction
  • Repeat pattern

Strategy 3: Harvest Tax Losses

Tax-loss harvesting involves selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains and up to $3,000 of ordinary income.

How It Works:

  1. Sell investments that have declined in value
  2. Use losses to offset capital gains
  3. Excess losses can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income
  4. Remaining losses carry forward to future years

Wash Sale Rule:

You can't claim a loss if you buy the same or "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. To maintain market exposure, you can:

  • Buy a similar but not identical investment
  • Wait 31 days and repurchase
  • Buy more of the same investment 31 days before selling

Strategy 4: Optimize Capital Gains

Capital gains tax rates are more favorable than ordinary income rates:

2024 Long-Term Capital Gains Rates:

Tax Rate Single Married Filing Jointly
0% Up to $47,025 Up to $94,050
15% $47,026 - $518,900 $94,051 - $583,750
20% Over $518,900 Over $583,750

Strategies:

  • Hold investments over one year to qualify for long-term rates
  • Time sales in low-income years (retirement, sabbatical, job loss)
  • Donate appreciated stock instead of cash to avoid capital gains
  • Use qualified opportunity zones for deferred gains

Strategy 5: Claim All Available Credits

Tax credits directly reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar, making them more valuable than deductions.

Common Tax Credits:

Credit Maximum Amount Eligibility
Child Tax Credit $2,000 per child Children under 17
Earned Income Credit Up to $7,830 Low to moderate income
American Opportunity $2,500 per student First 4 years college
Lifetime Learning $2,000 per return Any higher education
Saver's Credit $1,000 ($2,000 MFJ) Low-income retirement savers
Child and Dependent Care $3,000-$6,000 Childcare expenses

Strategy 6: Consider Roth Conversions

Converting traditional IRA or 401(k) funds to Roth can be strategic in low-income years:

When Roth Conversions Make Sense:

  • You're in a lower tax bracket than you expect in retirement
  • You have years before claiming Social Security
  • You have deductible business losses
  • You want to reduce future RMDs
  • You want tax diversification

How to Do It:

  1. Calculate how much you can convert while staying in your current bracket
  2. Convert traditional IRA/401(k) funds to Roth
  3. Pay taxes on the converted amount
  4. Enjoy tax-free growth and withdrawals

Strategy 7: Optimize Business Structure

If you're self-employed or own a business, your entity choice affects your taxes:

Sole Proprietorship vs. LLC vs. S-Corp:

Structure Best For Tax Benefits
Sole Proprietorship Simple, low-risk businesses Simple, 20% QBI deduction
LLC Most small businesses Liability protection, flexibility
S-Corp Profitable businesses ($60K+) Save on self-employment tax

Business Deductions to Claim:

  • Home office expenses
  • Vehicle mileage (67 cents/mile in 2024)
  • Health insurance premiums
  • Retirement plan contributions (SEP-IRA, Solo 401k)
  • Professional development and education
  • Business meals (50% deductible)
  • Software and subscriptions

Strategy 8: Time Your Income and Deductions

If you have flexibility in when you receive income or take deductions, time them strategically:

Defer Income:

  • Ask for year-end bonus to be paid in January
  • Delay invoicing clients until next year
  • Hold appreciated investments past one year

Accelerate Deductions:

  • Make charitable donations before year-end
  • Pay property taxes in December instead of January
  • Prepay deductible business expenses
  • Max out retirement contributions

Strategy 9: Use Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

If you're 70½ or older, you can donate up to $105,000 directly from your IRA to charity:

  • Counts toward your RMD
  • Not included in taxable income
  • Better than taking the distribution and then donating (especially if you don't itemize)

Strategy 10: Hire a Tax Professional

While DIY tax software works for simple returns, consider hiring a CPA or EA if:

  • You own a business
  • You have complex investments
  • You experienced major life changes (marriage, divorce, inheritance)
  • You want proactive tax planning, not just compliance
  • Your time is worth more than the fee

A good tax professional often pays for themselves by finding deductions and strategies you'd miss.

Tax Planning Checklist

Use this checklist each year:

  • ☐ Max out 401(k) contributions
  • ☐ Max out IRA contributions
  • ☐ Max out HSA if eligible
  • ☐ Harvest tax losses before year-end
  • ☐ Bunch deductions if beneficial
  • ☐ Make charitable donations
  • ☐ Review withholding and estimated payments
  • ☐ Consider Roth conversions in low-income years
  • ☐ Claim all eligible credits
  • ☐ Optimize business deductions
  • ☐ Time income and deductions strategically

Key Takeaways

  • Maximize tax-advantaged accounts first—these offer the biggest benefits
  • Understand the difference between deductions (reduce taxable income) and credits (reduce tax owed)
  • Time capital gains to take advantage of lower long-term rates
  • Harvest tax losses to offset gains
  • Consider Roth conversions in low-income years
  • Don't miss valuable tax credits, especially for education and children
  • If self-employed, optimize your business structure and deductions
  • Consider hiring a professional for complex situations

Tax planning isn't about finding loopholes—it's about understanding the rules and using them to your advantage. The strategies in this guide are completely legal and designed to help you keep more of what you earn. Start implementing them today, and you could save thousands on your next tax bill.
