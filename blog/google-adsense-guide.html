Why I Wanted AdSense (And Why You Might Too)

Look, I'm not gonna sit here and pretend I started this blog purely out of the goodness of my heart. I mean, yeah, I genuinely wanted to help people. But I also wanted to see if I could make some passive income from it. After paying off my debt and getting my finances in order, I had this nagging feeling that my blog could actually generate some revenue.

So in late 2024, I applied to Google AdSense. I figured I had decent traffic, good content, and a clean site design. How hard could it be?

Turns out, pretty hard. I got rejected. Not once, but twice.

Both times, the rejection email was frustratingly vague. "Your site is not ready to display Google ads," it said. No specifics. No actionable feedback. Just a door slammed in my face.

After a lot of head-scratching and reading through every forum post I could find, I figured out what was wrong. I fixed those issues, waited a bit, and applied again. This time? Approved in about 48 hours.

This guide is everything I learned the hard way. If you're thinking about monetizing your blog or website with AdSense, save yourself some frustration and read this first.

What Actually Is Google AdSense?

Google AdSense is basically Google's advertising program that lets you put ads on your website. When someone visits your site and clicks on or views one of those ads, you get paid. It's Google's way of connecting advertisers who want to reach your audience with you, the website owner who has that audience.

Think of it like this: a company wants to sell their financial planning software. They create an ad and tell Google they want to pay $2 every time someone clicks on it. Google then shows that ad on websites like mine that are part of the AdSense program. When you click the ad, the company pays Google, and Google gives me a cut.

Why do advertisers love it? They only pay when someone actually engages with their ad. Why does Google love it? They take a chunk of every transaction. Why do publishers (that's us) love it? We get paid for content we were already creating anyway.

The beauty of AdSense is that it's completely hands-off after you set it up. Google handles finding advertisers, serving the ads, processing payments, and dealing with refund requests. You just provide the space and wait for the checks to arrive.

How the Whole Thing Works

Let me break down the actual mechanics so you understand what's happening behind the scenes:

The Auction System

Every time someone loads your page, a real-time auction happens in milliseconds. Advertisers bid on the right to show their ad to YOUR specific visitor. Google looks at factors like:

  • How much the advertiser is willing to pay
  • Whether your content is relevant to their product
  • How likely the ad is to get clicked based on historical data
  • The visitor's location, device, and browsing history

The highest bidder wins, their ad gets shown, and you earn money based on either clicks or impressions (views).

CPC vs CPM

You'll hear these terms all the time:

  • CPC (Cost Per Click): You earn a specific amount every time someone clicks an ad. This is how most ads work. My average CPC in the personal finance niche is somewhere between $0.50 and $3.00, depending on the ad.
  • CPM (Cost Per Mille): You earn a set amount for every 1,000 ad impressions, regardless of whether anyone clicks. This is less common for smaller sites but happens with bigger publishers.

The Payment Process

Google pays you on a monthly schedule, but there's a threshold: you need to earn at least $100 before they send a check or do a bank transfer. I remember hitting $100 for the first time and feeling like I'd won the lottery, even though it had taken about four months of blogging.

Payments go out around the 21st of each month. If your balance is below $100 at the end of the month, it rolls over to the next month. This is called "payment threshold carryover," and it'll keep rolling until you finally hit that $100 mark.

My Two Rejections (And What I Learned)

Let me save you some time by sharing exactly why I got rejected, twice:

Rejection #1: Not Enough Content

My first application was submitted when I had maybe 8 blog posts, most of which were under 800 words. I thought "quality over quantity" would save me. Google disagreed. Their guidelines specifically state you need "substantial content" before they'll approve you, and 8 short posts apparently doesn't cut it.

What I learned: More content is better. Shoot for at least 20-30 substantive articles before applying. Each post should be at least 1,000 words of actual value, not fluff.

Rejection #2: Policy Violations

After building up more content, I applied again. Got rejected again. This time I was more confused because I'd grown the site significantly. Looking back at my old posts, I think I had some issues with:

  • Links to affiliate products that weren't properly disclosed
  • Some pretty aggressive CTA (call-to-action) buttons that Google might have flagged
  • A "contact" page that was just an email address with no actual contact form

Google's content policies are strict. They don't want ads on sites with:

  • Plagiarized or scraped content
  • Hate speech, violence, or adult content
  • Gambling or casino-related content
  • Weapons or explosives instructions
  • Copyrighted material you don't have rights to
  • Deceptive design patterns (think "click here to claim prize" stuff)

What I learned: Read Google's content policies. Yes, they're long and boring. Read them anyway. I went through mine with a fine-tooth comb after that second rejection and found several things to fix.

What You Actually Need to Get Approved

Based on my experience and a lot of forum research, here's the reality of what Google wants to see:

The Basics (Non-Negotiables)

  • Your own domain: No subdomain hosting (like yourblog.blogspot.com). You need your own domain (yourblog.com). This usually costs $10-15 per year through a registrar like Namecheap or Google Domains.
  • Active, original content: At least 20-30 quality posts, each with 1,000+ words of unique content. No AI-generated slop, no spun articles, no copying from other sites.
  • Working contact information: Either a contact form, an email address, or both. They want to know there's a real person behind the site.
  • Clear navigation: Visitors should be able to find their way around your site without getting lost. A logical menu structure is essential.
  • Privacy policy: This is mandatory. Your privacy policy needs to explain what data you collect, how you use cookies, and how users can opt out. I used a free generator and customized it for my specific situation.
  • Terms of Service page: Not technically required by Google, but having one makes your site look more legitimate. Keep it simple—just state the basic rules for using your site.

Content Requirements (Where Most People Fail)

  • Original content only: Google has gotten really good at detecting AI-generated content. Write your own stuff, or at minimum heavily edit AI drafts until they don't sound robotic.
  • Substantial content: Each page should have meaningful content, not just "Welcome to my blog. Check back soon for updates!"
  • Consistent publishing schedule: Sites that haven't been updated in months look abandoned. Google wants to see you're actively maintaining your site.
  • Proper categorization: Organize your content so it's easy to navigate. Tags, categories, a logical menu—these all help.

Site Quality (The Stuff Nobody Talks About)

  • Fast loading times: Google hates slow sites. Use a good hosting provider, optimize your images, and consider a CDN if you're serving international traffic.
  • Mobile-friendly design: More than half of web traffic is mobile. If your site looks broken on phones, you're gonna have a bad time with AdSense approval.
  • Clean HTML: Broken code, missing closing tags, malformed CSS—these can all trigger rejections. Run your site through a validator just to be safe.
  • No intrusive popups: Those "Subscribe to our newsletter!" popups that appear the second someone lands on your page? Google hates them. Wait until users have been on the page for a bit, or make them dismissible.

The Approval Process Walkthrough

Once you've got everything in place, applying is actually pretty straightforward:

Step 1: Create Your AdSense Account

Go to google.com/adsense and sign up with a Google account. You'll need to provide your website URL and some basic information about yourself or your business.

Step 2: Add the AdSense Code to Your Site

After submitting your application, Google will give you a piece of code to add to your website's HTML. This code goes in the <head> section of every page on your site. Most CMS platforms (WordPress, Wix, Squarespace) make this easy through plugins or built-in settings.

Step 3: Wait for Review

This is the hard part. Google's review process typically takes 24-48 hours, but it can take up to two weeks in some cases. They say the review is automated, but I'm convinced there's some human element in there somewhere because my second rejection came right after a weekend.

Step 4: Get Approved (Finally)

Once approved, you'll get an email saying "Congratulations!" At that point, you can log into your AdSense dashboard, create ad units, and start customizing how ads look on your site. It'll take another day or two for the ads to actually start appearing, since Google's system needs to crawl your pages and figure out which ads are relevant.

Step 5: Set Up Payment Settings

Before you start raking in the big bucks (kidding, mostly), you'll need to set up how you want to get paid. Options include:

  • Direct deposit (ACH): Fastest option if you have a US bank account. Takes 2-4 business days.
  • Wire transfer: Available internationally but has higher fees, usually around $15-25 per transfer.
  • Check by mail: Slow and requires going to the bank, but some people still prefer it.

Stuff I Wish I Knew Earlier

Here's some practical advice that would've saved me a lot of headaches:

Wait Until Your Site Is Really Ready

I know it's exciting to launch and start making money, but applying too early is the #1 mistake people make. Getting rejected looks bad on your account, and subsequent applications get extra scrutiny. Do it right the first time. Build out 25-30 solid posts, make sure everything is polished, then apply.

Don't Obsess Over Traffic Numbers

Contrary to popular belief, you don't need massive traffic to get AdSense approved. I've seen reports of people getting approved with less than 100 monthly visitors. That said, more quality content and some traffic definitely helps your case. Don't apply the day after launching your blog.

Get Your Legal Pages Sorted BEFORE You Apply

Privacy policy, terms of service, about page, contact page—these should all be done before you even think about AdSense. I put mine off and then had to rush to create them, which looked sloppy.

Don't Use Copyrighted Images

I used to grab images from Google Images all the time for my blog posts. Bad idea. Those are someone else's property. Use royalty-free image sites like Unsplash, Pexels, or Pixabay instead. It's not worth the potential takedown notice or policy violation.

Clean Up Your Navigation

Make sure your menu actually works and leads somewhere. Dead links and 404 errors are red flags. I went through my entire site a week before applying and fixed like 15 broken links I didn't even know existed.

Be Patient After Approval

When you first get approved, the ads might look weird or not be very relevant. This is because Google's system is still learning about your site. Give it 2-4 weeks to optimize. The more traffic you get, the smarter the ad targeting becomes.

Other Ways to Make Money From Your Site

AdSense isn't the only game in town. Here's a quick rundown of alternatives:

Affiliate Marketing

This is where you promote other companies' products and earn a commission when someone buys through your link. Amazon Associates is the most popular starter program. In personal finance, this could mean recommending credit cards, budgeting apps, or financial products. Commissions range from 3% to 50%+ depending on what you're selling.

Sponsored Posts

Companies pay you to write about their product or service. Rates vary wildly based on your traffic and niche. A finance blog with 10K monthly visitors might charge $200-500 per sponsored post. I get maybe one inquiry every few months, so it's not reliable income, but it's nice when it happens.

Digital Products

Ebooks, online courses, templates, presets—the sky's the limit here. The upside is you keep 100% of the revenue (minus payment processor fees). The downside is you have to create the product and handle customer service.

Direct Ad Sales

Once your site is big enough, you can sell ad space directly to advertisers, cutting out Google entirely. This typically requires at least 50K+ monthly visitors and takes a lot of sales effort, but the rates are usually much better than AdSense.

Is AdSense Worth It? My Take

After running AdSense on MoneyMind for about 18 months now, here's my honest assessment:

The good: It's zero work once set up. I've made roughly $2,400 from AdSense over the past year and a half. That's not quit-your-job money, but it's enough to cover my hosting costs and have a bit left over for coffee. The passive income aspect is real.

The not-so-good: AdSense revenue is incredibly inconsistent. Some months I make $300. Other months I make $30. It depends on so many factors—advertiser demand, the time of year (holiday season tends to pay more), what topics are trending, even the weather sometimes.

I'd still recommend AdSense for most bloggers because:

  • It's the easiest monetization method to set up
  • Google handles all the complicated stuff
  • It doesn't require any ongoing maintenance
  • Payments are reliable (once you hit the threshold)

But if you're serious about making money from your blog, don't put all your eggs in the AdSense basket. Build an email list, explore affiliate marketing, and consider creating digital products. Those income streams tend to be more stable and scalable.

If you've been thinking about applying for AdSense, my advice is simple: build a quality site first, read the policies carefully, and don't rush it. Your bank account will thank you later.
